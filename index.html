Market Regime tells you what KIND of market you're in right now — this determines which strategies work and which fail.

THE THREE REGIMES
RegimeGEX ValueMarket BehaviourBest Strategy
SHORT GAMMANegative (−)Dealers amplify every move. Big candles, momentum, trends. Breakouts hold.Breakout trades, momentum, directional options buys
LONG GAMMAPositive (+)Dealers suppress every move. Tight range, reversals, mean-reversion. Breakouts fail.Short straddle/strangle, iron condor, sell premium
TRANSITIONINGNear zeroRegime is flipping. Unpredictable. Wait for clarity.Reduce size, wait, no new positions
HOW TO USE THIS

Step 1: Check regime before entering any trade. Never trade breakout in LONG GAMMA — it will fail.
Step 2: Zero-Gamma level = the price where regime flips. If spot crosses zero-gamma, regime may change.
Step 3: In SHORT GAMMA, set wider stops (moves are bigger). In LONG GAMMA, set tight targets (price snaps back).

WHAT CAUSES REGIME CHANGES

As option sellers (dealers) write more puts than calls → GEX goes positive (LONG). When large directional buying in calls/puts overwhelms dealer capacity → GEX goes negative (SHORT). Near expiry, GEX typically collapses toward zero → regime becomes unpredictable near Thursday close.

Implied Volatility (IV) is the market's expectation of future price movement embedded in option prices. The IV Smile shows how IV varies across different strikes.

READING THE IV SMILE

Normal Smile (U-shaped): IV is highest at far OTM strikes on both sides. This is the typical shape when markets are calm — tails are priced as expensive insurance.

Volatility Skew (PE IV > CE IV): Higher IV on puts than calls = market fears downside more than upside. This is the most common shape for Indian indices. A large skew means put premiums are expensive.

Inverted Skew (CE IV > PE IV): Unusual — indicates market expecting upside move. Bullish. Rare in indices, sometimes seen in individual stocks.

IV Rank = (Current IV − 7-day Low IV) ÷ (7-day High IV − 7-day Low IV) × 100
